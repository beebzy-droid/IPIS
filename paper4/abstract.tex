Soft sensors, prognostic models, and real-time optimization (RTO) each increasingly
ship distribution-free coverage guarantees. Safe closed-loop operation, however,
turns on a joint event that none of them addresses alone: the product must stay in
specification while the equipment survives to its next maintenance window. That joint
guarantee does not follow from the three taken in isolation, and feedback is widely
believed to void it by breaking exchangeability. We derive a per-cycle \emph{composed}
coverage certificate for the joint safety event, with terms that map one-to-one onto
the three modules. A causal-timing argument then shows that the conformal-selection
penalty is exactly zero whenever the decision is formed before the measurement it is
conditioned against, which dissolves the feedback objection. A corollary collapses the
certificate into a single convex similitude-departure budget that a modifier-adaptation
RTO enforces directly, so the optimizer cannot push economics hard enough to invalidate
its own sensor and life guarantees; the flagship guarantee and the operating floor
become the same constraint. On a calibrated debutanizer digital twin running the real
soft-sensor, prognostic, and RTO implementations, the certified floor
$1-(\alpha_1+\alpha_2)-\varepsilon = 0.75$ is met empirically: joint safety coverage is
$0.988$ ($95\%$ interval $[0.976, 0.994]$) when the budget is enforced and $0.000$ when
it is not. Reflux-pump life carries the entire contrast, with remaining-life coverage of
$1.000$ against $0.013$. None of the three guarantees implies system safety on its own;
the composition is what the certificate provides.
