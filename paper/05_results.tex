\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Feature philosophy and model selection (C2, C3; debutanizer)}

Table~\ref{tab:t1} / Figure~\ref{fig:f2} report the feature ablation under the identical blocked
time-series CV protocol (5 folds, transport lag 15, plain linear regression, scaler fit
per fold-train). The four arms differ only in where model complexity comes from.

Three observations. First, the thermodynamically grounded set (bubble-point estimate,
relative volatility $\alpha$(T), stripping factor) dominates on every metric while using 4
features instead of 126: complexity set by physics beats complexity set by validation.
Second, the kitchen sink's failure is not a cross-validation artifact, because its held-out
test $R^2$ (+0.221) is less than half the deployed set's (+0.476), so the overfitting is
visible out-of-sample as well. Third, the negative folds in \emph{every} arm are the
cross-regime selection lottery that motivates the protocol: under naive adjacent
validation these regime shifts are averaged away and selection becomes seed-dependent;
under blocked CV they surface as the per-fold spread that the one-standard-error rule
then acts on. The deployed arm's row is bit-identical to the static row of the
independent bias-update evaluation (Section 5.2), an internal consistency check across
two separately written evaluation scripts.

The transport-lag diagnosis precedes all of this: at lag 0 the physics bridge appears
to fail (static $R^2$ $\approx$ 0.02), which a naive reading would call a physics error; lag
scanning recovers u5 at lag 15 as a stable interior maximum ($r^2$ $\approx$ 0.51). The lag-zero
failure is diagnostic of transport delay, not of the thermodynamics (see the accompanying repository).

\subsection{Drift handling under delayed labels (C3; debutanizer, TEP)}

Figure~\ref{fig:f4} shows the held-out residual trace before and after the open-loop bias-update
\citep{shardt2016} at the documented analyzer delay ($\lambda$ = 0.1 CV-selected on the pool, test
untouched; $\theta$ = 4). The static model's per-fold $R^2$ spread of {[}+0.224, \textbf{--1.494},
+0.574, +0.728, +0.695{]} (CV +0.145 $\pm$ 0.419) collapses to {[}+0.614, +0.487, +0.684,
+0.704, +0.751{]} (CV +0.648 $\pm$ \textbf{0.046}), a 9$\times$ reduction in cross-fold standard error,
which is the robustness claim: the update rescues the regime-shifted fold (--1.49 $\to$
+0.49) without sacrificing the good ones. On the held-out test window the same
mechanism moves $R^2$ from +0.476 to \textbf{+0.857} ($\Delta$ +0.381). The correction also exceeds
the best \emph{constant} per-fold offset (oracle CV +0.540), confirming that within-fold
drift tracking, and not merely offset removal, is doing work.

The same recipe applied unchanged to the TEP regimes reproduces the signature
(blocked-CV --0.089 $\pm$ 0.493 static; bias-update recovery to CV +0.19 at $\theta$ = 5, +0.45 at
$\theta$ = 2). The methodology therefore transfers across process topology before any model migration
is attempted (see the accompanying repository).

\subsection{Calibrated uncertainty under drift and delay (C4; TEP, synthetic)}

Static split conformal \citep{papadopoulos2002} assumes exchangeability; process drift
breaks it. On a controlled synthetic stress test (residual scale $\times$3 mid-stream, target
0.90), split coverage collapses to 0.403 while ACI \citep{gibbs2021aci} holds 0.899
and EnbPI \citep{xu2021enbpi} recovers with FIFO lag (0.832), as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:f6}. The real TEP
regimes exhibit the production version of this failure: not a collapse but
\emph{inconsistency} (Table~\ref{tab:t2} / Figure~\ref{fig:f7}).

The static baseline swings from under-coverage (0.847, modes 1--2) to over-coverage
(0.957, mode 3). It is invalid in both directions, and the over-covering regime pays for its
nominal-looking number with the widest intervals (3.18). The corrected + ACI
construction is regime-uniform at the target, with a coverage range of {[}0.897, 0.903{]} across all
six regime $\times$ delay cells at widths 1.7--2.5, and is robust to the label delay ($\theta$
enters through the bias-update; ACI follows the \S 3.4 protocol, $\gamma = 0.05$). EnbPI
sits between the two, structurally limited by its FIFO residual window on these
mild-drift regimes (its batch-size parameter is flat here: coverage 0.897--0.910 over
s $\in$ \{1, 10, 25, 50\}).

\subsection{Model migration across operating regimes (C5; TEP)}

Migration is evaluated as within-process regime migration (mode 1 $\to$ modes 2, 3) under
the two-layer composition (offline migration plus the Section 5.2 online bias-update,
$\lambda$ = 0.3, $\theta$ = 2) with n = 8 random-subset repeats per data fraction; literal
cross-process parameter migration is mathematically inapplicable here because the
source and target input spaces are disjoint (Section~5.4; full analysis in the accompanying repository). The metric is \textbf{data
efficiency}: the ratio of data fractions at which from-scratch and migrated models
reach 90\% of the from-scratch ceiling. (The stricter ceiling-crossover statistic
flipped between 20\% and 100\% across seeds in preliminary runs and is not headlined.)

Figure~\ref{fig:f5} shows the result. Yan functional scale-bias correction \citep{yan2011}, a per-input affine plus a
zero-mean GP bias on the source predictor, reaches 90\% of the ceiling at \textbf{5\%} of
target data versus \textbf{50\%} from scratch on \emph{both} targets: \textbf{10.0$\times$ data efficiency},
with calibrated 95\% GP posterior intervals (coverage 92--98\%) that are appropriately
wider for the larger mode-3 regime gap (widths 2.84--3.96 vs 2.65--3.16). Luo matrix
scale-bias correction \citep{luo2015} yields exactly 1.0$\times$ on both targets: for a linear
source its per-input scaling absorbs the source weights and the method is from-scratch
regression in disguise: the migrated and from-scratch curves coincide to $\pm$0.001--0.006
at every fraction, the empirical face of an identity we also verify analytically.
Output-only correction \citep{lu2008similarity} lands between (1.0$\times$ / 4.0$\times$). A bare-migration control
(no bias-update layer) caps every migrated curve below the 90\% threshold on mode 2:
the two-layer composition is load-bearing, because within-regime drift otherwise
swamps the cross-regime correction.

\subsection{The negative control: no physics anchors (C1; SECOM)}

SECOM inverts every assumption the framework exploits: 590 anonymized features over
1,567 samples (p $\approx$ n), 4.5\% missing cells, no thermodynamic structure. Framed as
virtual metrology (target x59, selected by stated criteria of missingness $\le$ 5\% and maximal
\textbar point-biserial r\textbar{} = 0.156 with pass/fail; audit table in the run output), the
elastic-net path under blocked CV explodes at weak shrinkage, with CV $R^2$ reaching $-7 \times 10^4$
with standard errors of the same magnitude (Figure~\ref{fig:f3}), the p $\approx$ n amplification of the
Section 5.1 lottery. The one-SE rule selects maximal shrinkage ($\alpha$* = 10, 6/441 nonzero
coefficients) and contains the damage; the resulting sensor still fails as a point
predictor: test $R^2$ = \textbf{--1.84} raw, --0.16 after the bias-update absorbs a large
calibration offset; even corrected, it cannot beat predicting the mean.

Coverage, however, survives (Table~\ref{tab:t3}): corrected + ACI delivers 0.910 / 0.915 ($\theta$ =
2/5) against the 0.90 target at mean width 12.8, where the static split over-covers
(0.953) at width 20.2, which is 37\% wider. \textbf{Conformal validity is model-agnostic; point
accuracy is what the physics anchors buy.} On a process where the sensor cannot be
made accurate, the framework still tells the truth about its own uncertainty. The
fail-enrichment of conformal misses is inconclusive (9 failing lots in the test
window) and is reported only as such.

\subsection{Real-time feasibility (C1, C4)}

The complete pipeline was exercised end-to-end against a live instance of the
online implementation (Figure~\ref{fig:f1}b). The measured latency per estimate on
commodity hardware, including communication and serialization (the number an
operator-facing system would observe), is 1.23~ms at the median and 1.97~ms at the
99th percentile over $10^4$ single-sample requests, with 32-sample batches amortizing
to ${\approx}46~\mu$s per sample and the delayed-laboratory-result update completing
in under 1~ms. A typical online-analyzer cycle of 200~ms is therefore met with two
orders of magnitude of headroom, and the margin is structural: the computation per
estimate is a linear map, an exponentially weighted average, and a quantile lookup.
Correctness under delayed laboratory results is enforced by construction: the
coverage indicator for a late result is computed against the interval \emph{recorded
when that sample was estimated} (Figure~\ref{fig:f1}b, dashed), not the interval the
since-adapted calibration would issue at arrival time, by which point $\alpha_t$
and the residual window have moved on. All software, evaluation scripts, and
provenance-stamped evidence artifacts accompany the paper (see Data and code
availability).
